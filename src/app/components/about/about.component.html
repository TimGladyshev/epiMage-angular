About

Aging is a complex biological phenomenon associated with the accumulation of molecular damage and other negative consequences of metabolism. One of these manifestations is altered methylation of cytosines within DNA. In 2013, Steve Horvath (UCLA) and then other scientists developed robust biomarkers of aging based on age-related changes in DNA methylation, known as epigenetic aging clocks. The original multi-tissue Horvath clock remains the most popular tool to assess the age of human cells, tissues and organisms. This clock has been shown to report the effects of rejuvenation associated with reprogramming of somatic cells to induced pluripotent stem cells, accelerated (breast) and decelerated (cerebellum) aging of certain human tissues, an increased age (compared to chronological age) associated with obesity, HIV infection and Down syndrome, and numerous other cases of age acceleration or deceleration associated with various conditions. As such, the Horvath clock reports changes in biological age, and therefore may be used to identify genetic and environmental conditions that modulate the rate of human aging. As of 2020, the original Horvath paper has been cited over 2600 times, and the associated clock has been used by hundreds of research groups. A major limitation, however, is the difficulty of using the clock by scientists without computational background. To address this deficiency, we provide a tool, based on the Horvath clock, that may use .csv files with beta values (DNA methylation levels at defined CpG sites) to quantify the age of human samples. The clock is based on methylation levels of CpG sites scattered across human chromosomes, each with defined weight in the overall model.

Samples are processed by scripts based on the wonderful library created by Daniel E. Martin-Herranz, and those from the original Horvath study. Check both out below.
